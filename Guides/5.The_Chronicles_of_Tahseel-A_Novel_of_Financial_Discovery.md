# The Chronicles of Tahseel: A Novel of Financial Discovery  
*An Unconventional Guide to Every Report in the Manage Portal — and the Human Stories Behind Them*

---

## Prologue  
In the gleaming towers of the Sharjah Digital City, a sprawling government portal hums with the heartbeat of a million transactions. It collects fees, loads cards, settles with banks, and — when the clock strikes month‑end — it must account for every fils. Yet behind every menu entry lies a tale of a frantic accountant, a suspicious auditor, a merchant who swears he was overcharged, and a citizen whose refund vanished into thin air.  

This is not a dry list of reports. This is a collection of detective stories, rescue missions, and financial thrillers. Each report in the Tahseel Manage Portal exists because someone, somewhere, faced a nightmare. And each report is the key that unlocked the solution.

Let us open the portal and step into the stories.

---

## Chapter 1: The Reconciliation Detective Agency  
*Reports: All Settlement and Bank Transfer Reports (Financial group)*

**The Crisis:** It’s the last day of the month. Fatima, the head of treasury, stares at two numbers that refuse to match. The bank says AED 12,540,210. Her system says AED 12,539,850. A discrepancy of 360 dirhams — small, but her auditor has no mercy. Somewhere, a transaction is duplicated, missing, or tampered with. She has 24 hours to find it.

**The Hero Reports:**  
She calls up the **Mashreq Settle Report**. It’s her magnifying glass. It lines up every internal transaction next to the bank’s records. Row by row, she scans for a mismatch. A green “Matched” flag appears beside most. Then — a red “Mismatch” on a credit card payment of 360 AED that the bank says was 0.00. The culprit: a voided transaction still present in the bank file. She then runs the **Mashreq bank settlement report in period** to confirm the pattern over the whole week, ensuring it wasn’t a systemic glitch.  

For the Sharjah Islamic Bank accounts, she deploys the **Bank Transfers of SIB** report to trace exact transfer details (masked account numbers, amounts, dates) and the **SIB Settlement Report** to reconcile the bank’s settlement file. The **General** version gives her the big picture across all SIB branches.  

When the Central Bank demands its monthly settlement, she generates the **UAE central bank settlement report**, confident that all mandatory fields are present and the totals match the external file. And if a dispute requires digging into every single record — including partial matches and format errors — the **Intensive Settlement Details Report** acts as the forensic lab, flagging every anomaly with a variance column.

**The Moral:** These reports exist because spreadsheets and manual checks fail. They turn reconciliation from a week‑long nightmare into a one‑click investigation.

## Chapter 2: The Tax Labyrinth  
*Reports: All Tax Reports (Tax group)*

**The Crisis:** Ali, a small business owner, submits his VAT return. He’s certain he charged 5% on all his government service fees. Yet the Federal Tax Authority sends him a penalty notice: his return shows a shortfall of 2,500 AED. He’s livid. “Your system miscalculated my taxes!” he shouts at the help desk.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The support agent, equipped with the **Transaction Details Tax Report**, pulls up every transaction from Ali’s company. In a clean table, she shows him: for each transaction, the base amount, VAT rate, VAT amount, and total. She filters by his company. A pattern emerges. Two services — “Environmental Compliance” and “Health Inspection” — are marked “Exempt” with 0% VAT. Ali had assumed everything was taxable. The numbers are correct; his own categorisation was wrong.  

To further reassure him, the agent runs the **Transaction Summary Tax Report**, which aggregates all transactions by tax rate, showing exactly how much was collected at 5%, 0%, and any other rate. She even generates a **Tax Invoice** for a sample transaction — a perfect, FTA‑compliant document with TRN and QR code — proving the system works flawlessly.  

For the government entities themselves, the finance team uses the **Transaction Details Tax Report – Revenue** to see the tax collected per revenue department, and the **Summary Tax Report – Revenue** to submit internal reports. They can prove that Sharjah Municipality’s share of VAT is correctly split from SEDD’s.

**The Moral:** Tax reports are not just about compliance; they’re about protecting reputations. They turn a taxpayer’s fury into a moment of clarity.

## Chapter 3: The Card That Wouldn’t Die  
*Reports: Tahseel Accounts Reports (P2 group)*

**The Crisis:** Rashid, a field collector, stands at a Tasheel center. A citizen hands him a Tahseel card to pay a traffic fine. Rashid swipes — “Insufficient balance.” The citizen insists, “I loaded 500 dirhams yesterday!” Rashid’s supervisor suspects a system error or, worse, fraud.

**The Hero Reports:**  
Rashid accesses the **Transaction Done by Tahseel Card** report. He enters the card number. The history pops up: a load of 500 AED yesterday, but then — a deduction of 499 AED at 8:02 PM for a different service. The citizen’s son used the card without telling him. Mystery solved.  

Meanwhile, the card operations manager uses the **Payment Card Statistic Report** to see how many cards are active, inactive, and lost across entities. When a card is reported stolen, she refers to the **Tahseel Lost Cards Report**, which tracks the old card, the replacement, and the transferred balance.  

To monitor fees collected from all card operations, the finance team runs the **Aggregated fees Report Tahseel Cards** and the **Tahseel Account Deduct & Load Request Report** to see the pipeline of pending loads and deductions. They can even spot a money loader who’s processing unusually high amounts — a red flag for audit.

**The Moral:** Cards are tiny vessels of money, but their trails are gigantic. These reports make every load, spend, and fee as visible as a ledger carved in stone.

## Chapter 4: The Great Refund Chase  
*Reports: Refund Reports (P2 group)*

**The Crisis:** Hessa, an elderly woman, paid for a business license renewal twice by mistake — once online, once at a kiosk. She’s been waiting three weeks for a refund. She calls the helpline, voice trembling.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The agent immediately opens the **Detailed Refund Report**, filters by Hessa’s transaction ID, and sees it: the refund request is “Pending Approval” with an approver who went on vacation. She escalates it. Once approved, the refund appears in the **Evoucher Refund Detailed Report** if it was an e‑voucher, or the **Refund loaded balance report on Tahseel card** if it was a card load refund.  

For bank‑processed refunds, the **Sharjah islamic bank refund report** confirms that the money left the government account and landed in Hessa’s bank. The **Automatic Transaction Refund Report** reassures them that the duplicate payment will be auto‑refunded next time without human intervention.

**The Moral:** Refunds are emotional. These reports don’t just track money — they restore trust.

## Chapter 5: The Revenue River  
*Reports: Revenue Reports (P2 group)*

**The Crisis:** The Director of Economic Development wants to know why his department’s revenue dropped 15% this quarter. The finance team scrambles.

**The Hero Reports:**  
They start with the **Detailed Transactions report by revenue entity**, filtering for SEDD. They see a sharp decline in “Trade License Renewal” transactions. But why? Switching to the **Total Transactions report by revenue entity**, they compare this quarter to last: volume is the same, but the average fee is lower.  

Digging deeper, they run the **Shared revenues between SEDD and SCTDA** report and discover that a new tourism‑promotion scheme redirected a portion of certain fees to SCTDA, reducing SEDD’s take. The **Service List Report** confirms the fee changes. The director now has an answer, not a mystery.

**The Moral:** Revenue reports are like a river’s tributaries — they show not just the flow, but where it diverges and why.

## Chapter 6: The Loading Dock Chronicles  
*Reports: Loading Reports (P2 group)*

**The Crisis:** An e‑voucher batch of 10,000 vouchers for “Sharjah Museums” was supposed to go live at 8 AM. At noon, visitors are still being charged. The museum manager is furious.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The operations team runs the **E‑Voucher Loading Report** and sees — nothing for that batch. They check the **Aggregated Loading Report of E‑Vouchers** and confirm zero. The batch never left the staging area. A quick fix, and the **Voucher Load Details Report** now shows each voucher as “Active.” The museum visitors flow in.  

Meanwhile, the **General Money Loading Report** reassures them that all other loading (accounts, cards) is normal. The **Daily Users Loading Report for Tasheel Centers** shows which operator loaded what, ensuring accountability.

**The Moral:** Loading is the fuel of the Tahseel engine. These reports ensure no empty tank goes unnoticed.

## Chapter 7: The Audit Ninja  
*Reports: Transactions Reports (P2 group)*

**The Crisis:** An auditor from the State Audit Institution arrives unannounced. He wants a full trail for a suspicious transaction that was created, cancelled, and then re‑created with a different amount — all within minutes.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The internal audit team pulls up the **Transaction Audit** report. It’s a time‑stamped log: 09:01 Created by User X, 09:03 Cancelled by same user with reason “error,” 09:04 New transaction created with a 10% lower amount. The auditor nods.  

They then run the **Detailed report of canceled transactions** to see how many cancellations this user performed this month. A pattern of “discount by cancellation” emerges, leading to an internal investigation. The **Recoverd Wallet Transactions Report** even shows an attempt to recover funds from a Tahseel account for that cancelled transaction — but it was rejected.

**The Moral:** Every transaction leaves a shadow. These reports shine a light on them.

## Chapter 8: The Communication Rescue  
*Reports: Communications Reports (P3 group)*

**The Crisis:** A field agent, Marwan, visits a company to resolve an issue with their payment terminal. The company later complains that no one ever came. Marwan swears he did.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The **Visits Report** saves Marwan. It logs his visit with timestamp, notes, and the company’s name. No debate. The **Issue tracking detail report** shows the case he raised after the visit — a faulty SIM card — and its resolution status. The complaint is withdrawn.

**The Moral:** In the world of field service, memory is unreliable. Reports are the impartial witness.

## Chapter 9: The Gatekeeper’s Log  
*Reports: System Reports (P3 group) & Truck Permissions (P3 group)*

**The Crisis:** A security breach — someone logged into the finance portal at 3 AM from an IP in a different country and exported a sensitive report.

**The Hero Reports:**  
The **Login Report** reveals the IP, username, and timestamp of every attempt. The rogue login stands out. The account is locked, and the incident is traced.  

For physical gates, the **Temporary Permission Report** and **Permanent Permission Report** track every truck that enters Sharjah ports. When a truck is involved in an accident inside the port, the **Permanent permission Log report** shows it was suspended a week ago but reactivated — without proper clearance. Someone will be held accountable.

**The Moral:** Whether digital or physical, gates must have logs. These reports are the keys to the kingdom’s security.
